Chapter 04 — What the Numbers Mean

So What Actually Happened?

The math gave us x₁ = 4 and x₂ = 1. But what does that mean for Ravi, Priya, and the passengers still waiting at the gate?

👨‍✈️

Ravi (x₁) goes from 2 → 4 shifts

Before the crisis, Ravi's domestic team handled 2 shift-units. After BOM-421 cancelled, the algorithm told the system: "We need more domestic coverage — and it's cheaper than international." So Ravi's team was scaled up to 4 shifts, absorbing the rerouted passengers.

x₁ = 4 means Ravi's domestic team now covers: Mumbai↔Pune, Delhi↔Jaipur, Mumbai↔Ahmedabad — routes that lost coverage when BOM-421 was grounded
+2 extra domestic shifts added
₹3 × 4 = ₹12 cost contribution
Covers rerouted domestic passengers
👩‍✈️

Priya (x₂) stays at 1 shift

Priya's international team stays at 1 shift — unchanged. The algorithm was smart: it didn't unnecessarily scale up the expensive international crew. The BOM-421 cancellation was a domestic disruption, so domestic crew (Ravi) absorbed the load. Priya's role is to hold the international minimum.

x₂ = 1 means Priya's team keeps the Mumbai↔Dubai route running — the one international route that absolutely could not be cancelled
No change from before crisis
₹5 × 1 = ₹5 cost contribution
Minimum international coverage held

Chapter 05 — The Theory

Why Dual Simplex?

Two methods, two different doors to enter. The one you pick depends on what broke. Here's the simple version.

Primal Simplex
🚪 Needs a valid starting solution
🔄 Restarts the entire puzzle fresh
⏱ Much slower for re-optimisation
📦 Throws away all previous work
✅ Great for brand new problems
VS
Dual Simplex ← We used this!
🔑 Needs dual-feasibility — we had it!
♻️ Reuses the existing basis/solution
⚡ Much faster for re-optimisation
💾 Preserves all prior computation
✅ Perfect when a constraint tightens
🧩

The Sudoku Analogy

You solved a Sudoku. Then someone changed one rule. Dual Simplex erases only the broken cells and fixes them — it doesn't throw the whole puzzle away. That's the entire insight.

Algorithm in 4 Steps

1. Find most negative RHS row → leaving variable.  2. Ratio test on that row → entering variable.  3. Pivot on the selected element.  4. Repeat until all RHS ≥ 0. Done.
